Syllabus

Description

Social media is in a pre-Newtonian moment, where we all understand that it works, but not how it works. --Kevin Systrom, cofounder of Instagram

"Es ist schon alles gesagt, nur noch nicht von allen." --Karl Valentin (Translation: It's been said before, but it hasn't yet been said by everyone.)

The number of active users of social media platforms such as Twitter, Reddit, Weibo (新浪微博), YouTube, and Facebook continues to grow. In light of this growth, interest in analyzing the data generated by users' interactions (often labeled "digital trace data") is considerable. This course prepares students to collect and analyze social media data using the Python programming language. Equal emphasis is placed on learning concepts and mastering the computing tools needed to apply them.

Course Goals

As a result of completing this course, the student should gain familiarity with different uses of social media data as well as with common methods used in the analysis of user-generated content. Students will also gain familiarity with the task of implementing some of these analyses using Python. Students will also develop an understanding of the varied social, political, and economic uses of social media data.

Textbooks

There are no required textbooks for this class. There are two recommended Python references. If you are new to Python you likely want one of them. Those who have no prior programming experience will likely want to acquire Automate the Boring Stuff with Python by Al Sweigart (ISBN: 978-1593275990). (The book is also available in HTML format online.) Those with some prior programming experience and those who enjoy mathematics may prefer Introduction to Computation and Programming Using Python by Guttag (ISBN: 9780262529624).

The following textbooks are also recommended for those interested in understanding in greater detail the range of methods discussed in the readings: Hand et al. (2001) Principles of Data Mining (ISBN: 9780262082907) and Newman (2010) Networks: An Introduction (ISBN: 9780199206650)

Schedule

This class meets in 3-hour sessions over a whole semester. The instructor will be available for office hours for 1 hour per week.

Prerequisites

Some programming experience is preferred but is not strictly required for this course. Basic Unix skills are required. For example, students should understand how to navigate the filesystem and edit text files. Students wishing to review these topics should consult Software Carpentry's "The Unix Shell".

Course Requirements

Readings: This course will be run in seminar style, and discussion and active involvement in the class activities will play an important role. The quantity and quality of contributions made to class count for 30% of the grade. Students are expected to read the assigned readings each week and to be prepared to discuss them in class. Readings associated with the second week of class, for example, should be completed before attending class.

Class Presentations: Students will present the assigned reading to the class, potentially in a group, twice over the course of the semester. We will form groups and each group will be responsible for presenting some of the papers we will be reading for the class. For your presentation, you are expected to read the papers and prepare slides to present them. Each of the two presentations is worth 10% of your final grade, for a total of 20%.

Final Project: Students are required to write a term paper. The term paper can be either (1) a literature review, an extensive survey (ca. 4000-6000 words) of previous work on one or more research areas featured in the course or (2) a technical report (ca. 3000-5000 words) describing the analysis of one or more social media corpora. (The technical report may also be a replication of a study we encountered during the course.) This analysis need not be focused on a task discussed in the course; it can be any task you are interested in, provided it relates to the analysis of social media data. Students will submit a one-page Final Project Proposal identifying the task/topic, research question(s), methods, and data on which the paper will be based. The final paper/report should follow the formal conventions for a publishable-quality research article, including footnotes and citations of scholarly work in ACL format (available from the ACL (see section "Format"; LaTex: acl2015.tex, acl2015.sty, acl2015.pdf, acl.bst; MS Word: acl2015.dot).

Final Project Proposal: Masters students must write a proposal of at least 750 words excluding references. PhD students must write a longer proposal (at least 1250 words, excluding references). If writing a literature review, the proposal should discuss the motivation for the project and identify specific research questions which will anchor the literature review. Projects involving data analysis must commit to using a specific dataset at the time of the proposal. The dataset selected and described in the proposal must be the same dataset used in the final project.

Assignments: You must complete programming assignments. These are graded on a pass/fail basis. All serious efforts will receive full credit.

Other: You are expected to check your email at a regular basis (e.g., at least twice between class meetings, including the morning before class).

Class Participation & Activities

Class discussion and activities are an important part of the course. Class participation means being willing and prepared to speak intelligently in class about the topics under discussion. In order to be able to speak intelligently about a topic, you will need to have done the readings for that topic. You will also need to be physically present and alert. In addition, to emphasize hands-on experience, you will be expected to use your computer/laptop to try out tasks as described in the syllabus.

Don't come to class if you feel unwell!

Reading Responses

The day before each class, post a short response to one or more of the assigned readings. You might comment on the organization of the paper or identify something that a paper does well (or poorly).

Class Presentations

You will be expected to present to the class, potentially in a group, twice over the course of the semester. We will form groups and each group will be responsible for presenting some of the papers we will be reading for the class. For your presentation, you are expected to read the papers well and prepare slides to present them.

Final Project

The term paper will be graded on content--originality of the research question, appropriateness of the data and SMM methods (and deployment therein in possibly working code) used to investigate the question, plausibility of your interpretations--and form--organization, clarity and quality of written expression, and appropriate use of scholarly conventions such as citations and footnotes. An 'A' quality term paper addresses an interesting research question, makes use of an appropriate empirical method to mine SMM data, and interprets the findings thoughtfully, in addition to being well-organized and clearly and professionally written and possibly accompanied by working code.

Assignments

Students come to this class with a wide range of skills and prior knowledge. In recognition of this, individual assignments will be graded in such a way that almost all valid submissions receive full credit. Assignments will typically be given an initial score using a scale ranging from 0 to 130. This score is not your grade. All submissions which demonstrate effort will receive full credit.
